# ✦ LOGO SUITE DESIGN DIRECTION ✦
### *Creative Director's Design Brief & Visual Identity System*
#### *For: ROSEMARY LEGOH & LEGOH ATELIER*
##### *Aesthetic Mandate: Modernist Architecture × Indonesian Wastra Heritage*

---

## PRE-DESIGN MANIFESTO

> *"A logo for a quiet luxury brand should do one thing above all else: feel inevitable. Not clever. Not decorative. Inevitable — as if it could not have been designed any other way."*

Before concepts, we establish the **two visual languages** that must coexist in tension:

| Modernist Architecture | Indonesian Wastra |
|---|---|
| Right angles, precise grids | Diagonal repeat patterns (parang, kawung) |
| Negative space as structure | Density as beauty |
| Swiss/Bauhaus typography | Hand-drawn irregularity, human imprecision |
| The column, the beam, the threshold | The fold, the thread, the knot |
| **Geometry as logic** | **Geometry as spirit** |

The mandate is not to *blend* these — it is to hold them in **productive tension**, the way a great building holds stone and light.

## CONCEPT R-1
# "THE FOLD LINE"
### *Logotype Concept One for Rosemary Legoh*

---

### 1) PRIMARY LOGOTYPE

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                             │
│                                                             │
│    R O S E M A R Y                                          │
│    ▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔▔                             │
│                          L E G O H                          │
│                                                             │
│                                                             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Typography Spec:
• "ROSEMARY"  — Custom wide-tracked serif, all caps
               Letterforms: slightly condensed, tall x-height
               The 'R' features a leg that extends horizontally
               beyond the letter boundary — like a structural beam

• "LEGOH"     — Same serif, but set LARGER (1.4× scale)
               Positioned flush-right, creating visual tension
               with "ROSEMARY" flush-left above

• Dividing Line — A single hairline rule (0.25pt) runs between
                  the two words, spanning the full width of "LEGOH"
                  This line is the JR FOLD translated into type
```

**Architectural Signature — "The Cantilever":**
The composition deliberately *unbalances* in a controlled way — "ROSEMARY" is set smaller and left-aligned, "LEGOH" is set larger and right-aligned, with the hairline bridging them. This mirrors a **cantilevered beam in architecture**: the smaller element supports the larger overhang through invisible structural tension. The asymmetry is *intentional and precise* — not accidental. It is the visual equivalent of the JR Fold Collar: structure that reads as elegance.

**Wastra Reference:**
The hairline rule echoes the *pola dasar* (base line) in batik making ��� the first line drawn on cloth from which all pattern radiates. It is the most invisible and most important element.

### 2) THE ABSTRACT MONOGRAM — "THE MARK"

```
        ┌──────────────────────────┐
        │                          │
        │         ╱╲               │
        │        ╱  ╲              │
        │       ╱    ╲             │
        │      ╱  RL  ╲            │
        │     ╱────────╲           │
        │    ╱          ╲          │
        │                          │
        └──────────────────────────┘

Construction:
• An equilateral triangle, but with the BASE made deliberately
  THICKER (3pt) than the two ascending sides (0.5pt)
• Inside: the negative space forms an abstract 'R' and 'L'
  in the counter-spaces between geometric subdivisions
• The thick base line = ground / foundation
• The thin ascending lines = aspiration / structure reaching upward
```

**Mark Rationale:**
The triangle is the most *structurally stable* form in architecture — every load-bearing system reduces to triangular forces. In Wastra, the *tumpal* (triangular border motif in batik and songket) appears at the threshold of sacred cloth — it is the motif of *transition and dignity*. This monogram sits at the intersection: **architectural stability + sacred threshold**. It reads as geometric and minimal at 5mm (sewn tag) but reveals its complexity at 50mm (hardware, dust bag). The hidden "R" and "L" reward close looking — the signature of quiet luxury.

## CONCEPT R-2
# "THE LOOM GRID"
### *Logotype Concept Two for Rosemary Legoh*

---

### 1) PRIMARY LOGOTYPE

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                             │
│                                                             │
│              R  O  S  E  M  A  R  Y                         │
│              |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |                         │
│              L  E  G  O  H                                  │
│                                                             │
│                                                             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Typography Spec:
• Both words set in GEOMETRIC SANS (custom)
  Letterforms based on perfect circles and straight lines only
  No curved transitions — all joints are RIGHT ANGLES
  (Like early Bauhaus type experiments)

• "ROSEMARY" set at same width as "LEGOH"
  Achieved by: tighter spacing on "ROSEMARY" (8 letters)
               wider spacing on "LEGOH" (5 letters)
  Result: Both words form a PERFECT RECTANGLE together

• Vertical hairlines ( | ) descend from each letter of
  "ROSEMARY" toward "LEGOH" — like warp threads on a loom
  The lines that don't meet a letter in "LEGOH" terminate
  at the midpoint — floating threads, incomplete weave

• This is the LOOM — the name is literally being woven
```

**Architectural Signature — "The Structural Grid":**
This logotype is a **visible grid made of language** — the two words become warp and weft, the type itself becomes textile. It references the **Miesian grid** (Mies van der Rohe's structural bay system, where all architecture submits to a perfect module) while directly quoting the *pakan* and *lungsi* (weft and warp) of Indonesian hand-weaving. The genius: the grid works even when you can't read the words — it has **visual logic independent of language**.

**Wastra Reference:**
*Tenun ikat* (Indonesian resist-dyed weaving) is built entirely on a grid system where threads are planned before dyeing. The logotype *is* a tenun plan — a design for something to be made.

```
        ┌──────────────────────────┐
        │                          │
        │      ┌───┐               │
        │      │   │               │
        │      │   └───────┐       │
        │      │           │       │
        │      └───────────┘       │
        │                          │
        │   (Interlocked R + L     │
        │    as architectural      │
        │    floor plan)           │
        │                          │
        └───────────���──────────────┘

Construction:
• The 'R' and 'L' are stripped to their STRUCTURAL ESSENCE:
  R → a vertical post + one horizontal beam (an inverted Γ shape)
  L → a vertical post + one horizontal base

• The two letterforms are INTERLOCKED at 90° — sharing one
  vertical member (the shared wall between two rooms)

• The result reads simultaneously as:
  → The letters R + L (initials)
  → An architectural floor plan (two interlocking rooms)
  → A weaving frame / loom structure
  → The abstract shape of a traditional Javanese 'pendopo' column
```

**Mark Rationale:**
This mark is a **typographic building** — it doesn't use the letters as decoration, it uses them as *load-bearing elements*. The shared vertical member is the critical detail: it says that "Rosemary" and "Legoh" (the person and the heritage) are structurally one — neither can stand without the other. At small scale (tag, hardware), it reads as a clean rectangular monogram. At large scale (storefront, packaging), it reveals the interlocking plan.

## CONCEPT R-3
# "THE INSCRIPTION"
### *Logotype Concept Three for Rosemary Legoh*

---

### 1) PRIMARY LOGOTYPE

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                             │
│   ROSEMARY                                                  │
│   ═══════════════════════════════════                       │
│                              LEGOH                          │
│                                                             │
│   ↑                              ↑                          │
│   Light weight,              Bold weight,                   │
│   tracked wide               condensed, powerful           │
│                                                             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Typography Spec:
• "ROSEMARY" — Ultra-light weight serif (hairline strokes)
               Wide letter-spacing (+200 tracking)
               Feels like an inscription etched into stone

• "LEGOH"    — Ultra-bold weight, same serif family
               Condensed (80% of normal width)
               Tight tracking — the letters press together
               like stones in an arch

• Double rule (═══) between them — NOT a single line
  Two rules: one 0.5pt above, one 0.5pt below, 2pt apart
  This double rule is the brand's typographic motif —
  it appears on hangtags, packaging edges, and stitching

• Weight contrast ratio: 9:1 (hairline to ultra-bold)
  This extreme contrast IS the aesthetic statement
```

**Architectural Signature — "The Inscription Stone":**
This logotype is designed to feel like it was **cut into a surface**, not printed on one. The extreme weight contrast between "ROSEMARY" (whisper) and "LEGOH" (declaration) mirrors the Indonesian tradition of **prasasti** (royal stone inscriptions) — where the name of the kingdom was cut deepest, and the description of the deed was carved finer. The double rule is the *frame of the stone* — it says: what is inside this line is *permanent*. This is not a trend. This is a record.

**Wastra Reference:**
In batik *tulis* (hand-drawn batik), the *canthing* tool creates lines of wildly varying weight — hairlines and bold strokes coexist in the same motif. The extreme weight contrast in this logotype is the *canthing* applied to typography.

### 2) THE ABSTRACT MONOGRAM — "THE MARK"

```
        ┌──────────────────────────┐
        │                          │
        │           ╱│             │
        │          ╱ │             │
        │         ╱  │             │
        │        ╱   │             │
        │       ╱    │             │
        │      ╱─────┤             │
        │             │            │
        │             │            │
        │                          │
        │  (Diagonal + Vertical    │
        │   = Abstract 'RL'        │
        │   + Architectural        │
        │   buttress form)         │
        │                          │
        └──────────────────────────┘

Construction:
• One BOLD vertical stroke (the L — column, standing)
• One DIAGONAL stroke meeting it at 60° (the R — leaning, reaching)
• A SHORT HORIZONTAL at the junction (the structural bracket)
• The diagonal references: the 'R' leg / the parang diagonal /
  the lean of a flying buttress / the angle of a kimono lapel
• The whole form reads as: RL monogram / structural bracket /
  parang batik diagonal / a person leaning on architecture
```

**Mark Rationale:**
This is the most **gestural and human** of the three marks — it captures movement within structure. The diagonal stroke has the quality of a **calligraphic gesture**: it was made by a hand, not a machine. Yet its meeting with the vertical is *exact* — the 60° angle is architecturally precise. This tension between the *made* and the *calculated* is the entire philosophy of the brand: Wastra heritage (hand) + Modernist structure (precision).

## CONCEPT LA-1
# "THE THRESHOLD"
### *Logotype Concept One for Legoh Atelier*

---

### 1) PRIMARY LOGOTYPE

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                             │
│         │                                                   │
│         │  L E G O H                                        │
│         │  ─────────────────────────────                    │
│         │        A T E L I E R                              │
│         │                                                   │
│                                                             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Typography Spec:
• A VERTICAL RULE (2pt, full height of both words) anchors
  the LEFT edge — this is the THRESHOLD LINE

• "LEGOH"    — Bold serif, all caps, set immediately right
               of the vertical rule (2pt gap only)
               The word presses against the line like
               a wall against a doorframe

• Thin horizontal rule separates "LEGOH" from "ATELIER"
  The rule does NOT extend to the left of the vertical rule
  It begins at the vertical rule and extends right

• "ATELIER"  — Regular weight, wide tracking, smaller scale
               Indented 1em from "LEGOH"
               Feels like a subtitle on a building plaque

• The vertical rule is the DOOR. LEGOH is above the threshold.
  ATELIER describes what happens inside.
```

**Architectural Signature — "The Doorframe / The Threshold":**
Every significant building in Indonesia marks its threshold — the *gapura* (ceremonial gate), the *pintu gerbang*, the *candi bentar*. This logotype IS a threshold: the vertical rule is the gate, and "LEGOH" is the name above the lintel. To read this logo is to *enter* the brand. This architectural gesture is borrowed from the **International Style** (think the clean vertical columns of Lever House, New York) but charged with Indonesian spatial meaning — the threshold as sacred transition.

**Wastra Reference:**
The *tumpal* pattern (triangular border) in batik and songket marks the threshold of cloth — the boundary between patterned and sacred space. This logotype places the brand name at exactly that boundary.

This is the most conceptually precise mark in the suite: it transforms two letters into a spatial experience. On hardware (buttons, clasps), it embosses as a clean architectural form. On the dust bag, the negative space rectangle becomes a *window* — a view into the garment inside.

## CONCEPT LA-2
# "THE BLUEPRINT"
### *Logotype Concept Two for Legoh Atelier*

---

### 1) PRIMARY LOGOTYPE

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                             │
│   ┌─────────────────────┐                                   │
│   │  L E G O H          │  ← Box drawn with 0.5pt rules    │
│   │  · · · A T E L I E R│    Right side open (no closing   │
│   └─────────────────────┘    vertical rule) — intentional  │
│                                                             │
│   ↑ "LEGOH" — Bold, confined   ↑ "ATELIER" — tracked,      │
│     within the box               lighter, escaping right   │
│                                                             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Typography Spec:
• A three-sided rectangle (left, top, bottom — right side OPEN)
  frames "LEGOH" in bold serif

• "ATELIER" in regular weight continues to the RIGHT
  of the open box — it EXITS the frame

• Three dots ( · · · ) in "LEGOH"'s baseline row
  precede "ATELIER" — like a blueprint measurement notation

• The OPEN RIGHT SIDE is critical: it says the brand is
  in process — the blueprint is alive, the work continues

• The three-sided frame references:
  → Architectural drawing notation (room labels)
  → Batik 'isen' fill pattern boundaries
  → A sample tag on a garment — the thing not yet finished
```

**Architectural Signature — "The Blueprint / The Technical Drawing":**
This logotype is a **working document** — it looks like it was pulled from an architect's or pattern-maker's drafting table. The three-sided frame is an architectural *room label* — the kind you find on construction drawings, where the room name sits in a rectangular callout. But the open right side subverts the closure — this is not a finished room, this is a *space being made*. For a brand named "Atelier" (a maker's studio), this is cosmically correct.

**Wastra Reference:**
In batik pattern design, the *pola* (pattern plan) is drawn on paper first, using ruled lines and notation marks that look almost identical to this logotype. The brand literally *shows its working* — the design process is the design.

```
        ┌──────────────────────────┐
        │                          │
        │                          │
        │       ┌───               │
        │       │                  │
        │       │                  │
        │       │                  │
        │       └───               │
        │                          │
        │  (The open-sided         │
        │   square — the           │
        │   'blueprint room        │
        │   label' reduced         │
        │   to pure form)          │

Construction:
• Three lines: one vertical (full height), one short horizontal
  at top, one short horizontal at bottom — the open 'C' rotated
• The proportions: top and bottom horizontals = 40% of
  vertical height — creating a form that is neither a 'C'
  nor a bracket, but something architecturally between
• Inside the open form: a single centered dot (·)
  representing the needle / the point / the origin
• The dot is the 'L' of Legoh reduced to its irreducible essence
```

**Mark Rationale:**
This is the most **abstract and conceptual** mark in the entire suite. It eschews legible letterforms entirely in favor of a pure architectural symbol. The three-sided form is simultaneously: a room label, a *selvedge bracket* (the edge marker on woven cloth), a measurement bracket in engineering drawing, and the letter 'C' (for *craft*, for *create*, for *continue*). The central dot grounds it — it is the *titik* (point) from which all batik patterns traditionally begin. Maximum minimalism, maximum meaning.

## FINAL CREATIVE DIRECTOR'S NOTE

> *The greatest logos in fashion are not symbols of a brand. They are the brand itself — compressed into the smallest possible space without losing a single atom of meaning.*
>
> *"Rosemary Legoh" carries three concepts that no competitor can own simultaneously: a woman's full name (human), structural innovation (architectural), and textile heritage (cultural). The logo system above does not illustrate these concepts — it IS these concepts, translated into line weights and negative space.*
>
> *Build these identities with patience. A logo earns its authority over years, not campaigns. The JR Fold Collar will be recognized before the logo is — and that is correct. Let the product lead. Let the mark follow. Let the name become inevitable.*
